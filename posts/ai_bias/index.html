When the Data Fights Back

How artificial intelligence can quietly learn to discriminate — and what it looks like when it does. Two interactive stories: predicting crime, and recognising faces at a border.

No maths required — just play with the controls and watch. Every figure here is synthetic and illustrative; the mechanisms are real, the numbers are made up. Curious readers can open the “Under the hood” panels for the method and the maths.

Scroll to begin ↓

The core idea

A machine that grades its own homework

Modern AI tools make predictions from past data, and those predictions decide what happens next — where police patrol, who gets stopped at a border. The catch: those actions then create the next batch of data the machine learns from. When a system shapes the very data it will be trained on, small, even random, imbalances can snowball. This self-reinforcing cycle is called a feedback loop.

You do not need to know any mathematics to see it happen. Below, a simple loop turns tiny differences into large, entrenched ones — the same mechanism that can lead to some neighbourhoods being over-policed while others are under-policed, regardless of where crime actually occurs.

Keep this loop in mind. In the first story we let you run it yourself on a city; in the second we show the same idea decides who a camera trusts.

Case 1 · Predictive policing

Sending patrols to the "right" places

Imagine a city divided into 25 areas. Suppose — and this is the crucial twist — that crime is actually spread evenly across all of them. A predictive-policing system does not see true crime; it only sees recorded crime. And crime is far more likely to be recorded where police already are: a patrol makes an offence several times more likely to be observed.

So the system learns from a distorted picture, sends patrols accordingly, and by doing so distorts the picture further. Press Play below and watch what a machine-learning model does to a perfectly fair city.

The lesson is stark: once a machine-learning model is in the loop, it amplifies small — even purely random — differences and ends up concentrating patrols in a handful of areas, even when crime is genuinely uniform. The simple, non-learning model does not do this. The bias is not in the data to begin with; the system manufactures it.

Under the hood: the method & the maths

The world (what actually generates crime). Each area \(i\) has a hidden true crime rate \(\Omega_i\), constant over time (uniform by default: \(\Omega_i = 1\)). Nobody observes it directly. What the system records each day mixes what the public reports and what police happen to observe:

$$ r_i^{(t)} \;=\; \underbrace{\alpha_i\,\Omega_i}_{\text{reported}} \;+\; \underbrace{V\,\Omega_i\,\big(1+(m-1)\,\rho(\beta_i)\big)}_{\text{observed}}\cdot\varepsilon $$

where \(\alpha_i\) is the reporting rate, \(V\) the base observability, \(m\) the patrol detection multiplier (a patrol makes an offence roughly \(m\times\) more likely to be seen), and \(\rho(\beta_i)=1-e^{-\beta_i P}\) the chance a patrol is present given patrol share \(\beta_i\) and \(P\) patrol units. \(\varepsilon\) is small multiplicative noise. Crucially, the model only ever sees \(r_i\) — never \(\Omega_i\).

What the model remembers. It learns from a recent-weighted record (an exponential moving average):

$$ R_i^{(t)} \;=\; \lambda\,R_i^{(t-1)} + (1-\lambda)\,r_i^{(t)} $$

The predictor. Real predictive-policing tools fit a classifier or regressor — typically naive Bayes or logistic regression — to historical records, estimating \(P(\text{crime}\mid \text{area})\). We reproduce the two behaviours that matter:

  • Simple (proportional): \(\hat p_i = R_i / \sum_j R_j\).
  • Machine learning (sharpening): \(\hat p_i \propto R_i^{\gamma}\) with \(\gamma>1\).

That exponent \(\gamma\) is the whole story. With \(\gamma=1\) the loop is stable. With \(\gamma>1\) the update has gain greater than one, so any small imbalance grows each cycle without bound — a runaway feedback loop. Logistic regression and naive Bayes trained on their own biased outputs behave the same way.

The loop. Patrols follow the prediction, \(\beta_i=\hat p_i\) (shares summing to 1). More patrols raise \(\rho(\beta_i)\), which raises observed crime \(r_i\), which raises \(R_i\), which raises \(\hat p_i\) — and round again.

Mitigations (the toggles) each attack one link:

  • Raise & equalise reporting: a large, equal \(\alpha\) makes the patrol-independent term \(\alpha\Omega\) dominate, anchoring records to reality.
  • Regularisation: pull the allocation toward uniform, \(\beta_i \leftarrow (1-\eta)\,\hat p_i + \eta/N\), which caps extreme values.
  • Downsampling: divide the observed term by its patrol inflation \(1+(m-1)\rho(\beta_i)\) to undo the sampling bias.

The drift meter is the total-variation distance between the (normalised) recorded distribution and the truth: \( D=\tfrac12\sum_i \lvert \tilde R_i-\tilde\Omega_i\rvert \). Zero means the data still matches reality.

This is a deliberately simplified model. Parameters (\(m,\gamma,\lambda\)) are chosen so the effect is visible within seconds, not to match any real force.

Case 2 · Border security

One threshold, unequal consequences

At a border, a camera compares each traveller's face against a watchlist and produces a similarity score. A single cut-off — the threshold — decides who gets pulled aside. It sounds neutral: the same number applies to everyone.

But face-matching systems do not work equally well for everyone. When one group's scores are naturally noisier, the very same threshold flags far more innocent people from that group. Move the slider and watch a single "fair" rule produce unfair outcomes.

Notice the link back to Case 1: the group flagged most often gets scrutinised most often. More scrutiny means more records, more suspicion — another feedback loop, now written onto people rather than places.

Under the hood: the method & the maths

The score. A face matcher compares two images and returns a similarity score \(s\). For a given demographic group \(g\), the scores form two bell curves — one for genuine comparisons (the same person, "mated") and one for impostor comparisons (different people, "non-mated"). We model each as a normal distribution with density

$$ f(x;\mu,\sigma)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\;e^{-\tfrac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} $$

so group \(g\) has impostor scores \(\mathcal N(\mu_I^g,\sigma_I^g)\) and genuine scores \(\mathcal N(\mu_G^g,\sigma_G^g)\). Groups whose curves overlap more are intrinsically harder.

The decision. Flag the traveller if \(s \ge \tau\), for one global cut-off \(\tau\).

The two errors, written with the normal cumulative function \(\Phi\):

$$ \mathrm{FMR}_g(\tau)=P(s\ge\tau\mid\text{impostor})=1-\Phi\!\left(\frac{\tau-\mu_I^g}{\sigma_I^g}\right) $$ $$ \mathrm{FNMR}_g(\tau)=P(s<\tau\mid\text{genuine})=\Phi\!\left(\frac{\tau-\mu_G^g}{\sigma_G^g}\right) $$

FMR is the false-match rate — an innocent person flagged. FNMR is the false-non-match rate — a real target missed.

Why one threshold is unfair. Because \((\mu_I^g,\sigma_I^g)\) differ by group, a single \(\tau\) yields a different \(\mathrm{FMR}_g\) for each. We report the disparity as

$$ \text{disparity}=\frac{\max_g \mathrm{FMR}_g(\tau)}{\min_g \mathrm{FMR}_g(\tau)} $$

Real-world grounding. Large biometric evaluations (for example, NIST's Face Recognition Vendor Test) repeatedly find false-match rates that vary by demographic — often markedly higher for darker-skinned and for female faces, and highest at their intersection. Our synthetic \((\mu,\sigma)\) reproduce that direction; the exact numbers are illustrative, not measured.

Mitigation — per-group calibration. Instead of one \(\tau\), pick a per-group threshold \(\tau_g\) that equalises the error, solving

$$ \mathrm{FMR}_g(\tau_g)=\text{target}\quad\text{for every } g $$

(done numerically by bisection). This levels the false-match rate across groups and drives the disparity toward \(1\times\).

Synthetic distributions, chosen for clarity. The point is the shape of the problem and its fix — not any specific vendor's accuracy.

Opening the black box

Making AI accountable

Both stories share a lesson: you cannot trust what you cannot inspect. Throughout the two demos you toggled four capabilities that turn an opaque system into one people can question. These are the building blocks of explainable, accountable AI.

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act classifies both predictive policing and remote biometric identification as high-risk (or, in some cases, prohibited) precisely because they can harm fundamental rights at scale. High-risk systems carry legal duties for transparency, human oversight, record-keeping and monitoring — the very things these panels make visible.

What helps

The loop can be broken

Bias here is not inevitable. Each of these levers, tested in the demos above, weakens the loop:

Keep humans in charge

Predictions are advice, not verdicts. People must be able to question, override and explain each decision.

Fix the data, not just the model

Improve trust and reporting in under-served areas; correct sampling bias (downsampling) so records reflect reality.

Restrain the model

Regularisation stops predictions from becoming extreme, blunting runaway loops before they form.

Calibrate per group

For biometric systems, tune thresholds so error rates are equal across groups — not just on average.

Watch for drift

Continuously compare what the model sees against reality. When they diverge, something is wrong.

Audit and trace

Keep records so any decision can be reconstructed, challenged and corrected after the fact.

None of this means abandoning artificial intelligence. It means keeping people in the loop — to question it, test it, and switch it off when it stops serving reality.

Plain-language glossary

Feedback loop
When a system's own outputs shape the data it later learns from, reinforcing whatever it already believed.
False positive (false match)
An innocent person wrongly flagged — e.g. a traveller mistaken for someone on a watchlist.
False negative (false non-match)
A genuine target the system misses.
Threshold
The cut-off score at which a system decides "yes" versus "no".
Overfitting
When a model latches onto patterns (even noise) in its training data instead of the real world.
Regularisation
A mathematical brake that discourages extreme predictions, reducing overfitting.
Data drift
When the data a model sees gradually stops resembling reality.
Explainability
Being able to say why a model made a particular decision.
